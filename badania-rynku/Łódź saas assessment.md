# **Strategic Assessment of the Polish B2B SaaS Market for Extracurricular Management: A Case Study of Łódź**

## **1\. Executive Summary**

This comprehensive research report evaluates the commercial viability, structural dynamics, and competitive intensity of the B2B SaaS market dedicated to managing paid after-school activities (PASA) in Poland. With a specific analytical lens focused on the Łódź metropolitan area, the report interrogates the core strategic question: Does this sector represent a lucrative "blue ocean" opportunity for a new platform, or is it a "tarpit"—a market segment characterized by high customer acquisition costs (CAC), low willingness to pay (WTP), and structural fragmentation that traps startups in a cycle of stagnation?

The analysis is grounded in data from the 2024/2025 school year, synthesizing demographic statistics from the Central Statistical Office (GUS), consumer spending trends from CBOS, and a deep competitive dissection of incumbents such as ActiveNow, SportsManago, and LangLion. The findings suggest a complex dichotomy. On the demand side, the market is robust; 5.3 million students populate the Polish educational system 1, and parental expenditure on school-related needs has surged to an average of 2,407 PLN per child annually, with 72% of parents enrolling children in paid extracurriculars.2 This indicates a strong flow of capital from parents to activity providers.

However, the supply side presents significant "tarpit" characteristics. The market is fragmented into distinct verticals—language schools, sports clubs, and coding academies—each with entrenched, specialized software incumbents. The "hair on fire" problems for these providers are administrative coordination and cash flow management. While a generic "booking marketplace" faces severe liquidity risks and low barriers to entry, the analysis identifies a viable pivot pathway: a "Vertical Fintech" operating system that monetizes through transaction flow and financial automation rather than pure SaaS subscriptions, mirroring the successful trajectory of players like LiveKid.4

## **2\. Macro-Environmental Analysis: The Polish EdTech Landscape**

To understand the potential of a B2B SaaS platform in Łódź, one must first establish the national context. The Polish market for after-school activities is not merely a collection of hobbies; it is a critical shadow education sector driven by demographic shifts, economic inflation, and changing parental aspirations.

### **2.1 Demographic Foundations and Student Population Dynamics**

The Total Addressable Market (TAM) for any ed-tech platform is ultimately capped by the student population. According to preliminary data for the 2024/2025 school year, the Polish education system serves approximately 5.3 million children and youth.1 This figure serves as the theoretical ceiling for end-users.

Within this aggregate, the primary school segment is the most commercially relevant. Primary school students, typically aged 7 to 15, represent the "Goldilocks" zone for paid activities: they are too young for complete autonomy but old enough for specialized skill acquisition (coding, competitive sports, foreign languages). In the 2024/2025 academic year, nearly 3 million pupils were enrolled in primary schools across the country.6

However, the market is facing a demographic headwind. Data indicates a year-over-year decline in the number of students, driven by broader depopulation trends in Poland. The number of primary schools decreased by 0.7% in the Łódź region specifically, and the intake of first-graders is shrinking.7 This demographic contraction fundamentally alters the value proposition of B2B software. In a growing market, providers need tools to manage *overflowing* capacity (scheduling, waitlists). In a shrinking market, providers need tools to *acquire and retain* students (marketing automation, CRM, retention tracking). Consequently, a platform entering the market in 2025 must prioritize features that drive revenue growth for its clients, rather than merely organizing existing logistics.

### **2.2 The Economics of Parental Investment**

The financial health of the B2B SaaS customer (the activity provider) is directly downstream of the parent's wallet. Despite inflationary pressures, the prioritization of education remains resilient. The CBOS "School Starter Kit" report for the 2024/2025 school year reveals a dramatic increase in spending. Parents spent an average of 2,407 PLN on school-related expenses, an increase of over 800 PLN compared to the previous year.2

This expenditure is not evenly distributed, creating distinct market tiers that a SaaS platform must address.

| Expenditure Tier (PLN) | % of Parents | Market Segment Characteristics |
| :---- | :---- | :---- |
| **0 – 300** | 2% | Non-Addressable. Likely reliant on free public/municipal programs. |
| **301 – 1000** | 43% | Mass Market. Community sports clubs, group English lessons. High volume, price-sensitive providers. |
| **1001 – 1500** | 14% | Mid-Market. Private language schools, specialized tutoring. Providers have moderate budgets for tech. |
| **Over 1500** | 28% | Premium. Elite coaching, robotics (e.g., Giganci Programowania), individual tutoring. Providers require high-touch, branded software. |

Source: Analysis of CBOS survey data.2

The data reveals a "hollowing out" of the low end; the percentage of parents spending very little has dropped, while the high-spending bracket (\>1500 PLN) remains robust at 28%. This suggests that despite the "tarpit" reputation of selling to SMBs, a significant portion of activity providers are generating substantial revenue per student, theoretically supporting a budget for professional management software.

### **2.3 The "300 Plus" Effect and Government Subsidies**

The market is partially buoyed by social transfers such as the "Dobry Start" (300 PLN) benefit. While intended for school supplies, this liquidity frees up household budget for extracurricular tuition. For a SaaS platform, this seasonality is critical: booking systems experience massive spikes in traffic and revenue in late August and September (the "Back to School" window), followed by a secondary spike in January/February (post-semester enrollment).1 The infrastructure must be built to handle these surges, while the business model must account for potential churn during the summer months (July-August) when regular classes pause.

## **3\. Case Study: The Łódź Metropolitan Area**

The city of Łódź serves as an ideal microcosm for this analysis. As a post-industrial city undergoing revitalization, it reflects the "median" Polish market—less affluent than Warsaw, but sufficiently dense to support a diverse ecosystem of private education providers.

### **3.1 Local Demographics and Educational Infrastructure**

In the Łódź Voivodeship, the educational landscape comprises hundreds of primary schools and a dense network of private providers. In the 2023/2024 school year, while the number of primary schools decreased slightly, the system still accommodated tens of thousands of students.8 The city’s strategy emphasizes educational development, and the density of the population in districts like Bałuty, Widzew, and Polesie creates "clusters" where after-school activities flourish.10

### **3.2 Provider Density and Categorization in Łódź**

Research into the Łódź market reveals a highly stratified supply side. Identifying the "Ideal Customer Profile" (ICP) is essential to avoiding the tarpit.

#### **3.2.1 The "Whales": Networked Franchises**

At the top of the food chain are large, networked academies. A prime example in Łódź is **Giganci Programowania**, located at Gdańska 126/128.11

* **Scale:** Part of a global franchise with 130,000 students.13  
* **Needs:** Complex curriculum management, teacher training tracking, multi-location reporting.  
* **Tech Stack:** High maturity. They likely use custom enterprise solutions or high-tier plans of platforms like LangLion or dedicated LMS.  
* **SaaS Opportunity:** Low. Selling a generic tool to a "Whale" is difficult as their requirements are bespoke.

#### **3.2.2 The "Dolphins": Established Local Schools**

This is the "sweet spot" for B2B SaaS. In Łódź, this segment is dominated by language schools and organized sports clubs.

* **British Centre:** A major language school with branches on Pomorska and Kościuszki streets.14 They run courses for children aged 2-12 and handle exams.15 Their administrative burden involves managing hundreds of students, exam registrations, and teacher schedules.  
* **ChlorekSwim & Pływające Ananasy:** These swimming schools operate out of municipal pools like "Wodny Raj" or "Aquapark Fala".16  
  * **Tech Reality:** These providers are *already* using SaaS. Specifically, **ActiveNow** is the dominant platform for swimming schools in Łódź. Their websites explicitly link to ActiveNow for registrations.16 This validates the market (they are paying for software) but indicates high barriers to entry due to switching costs.

#### **3.2.3 The "Minnows": Solo Instructors and Micro-Clubs**

The bottom of the market consists of individual tutors and small hobby clubs.

* **Examples:** Private guitar tutors advertising on Superprof 18, small art studios like "Szkicart" 19 or "Studio4".20  
* **Tech Reality:** These providers often rely on phone bookings, Excel, or simple aggregators like OLX.  
* **Tarpit Risk:** High. These users have high price sensitivity (often \<100 PLN/month budget for software) and high churn rates. Acquiring them is expensive relative to their Lifetime Value (LTV).

## **4\. Competitive Landscape and Technological Maturity**

To determine if a new platform is a viable pivot, we must rigorously analyze the current incumbents. The Polish market is not a "green field"; it is a crowded "red ocean" of vertical-specific solutions.

### **4.1 ActiveNow: The Horizontal Leader**

ActiveNow has established itself as the operational backbone for many "Dolphin" sized clients in Łódź, particularly in swimming and general activities.

* **Feature Set:** The platform covers the entire lifecycle: class scheduling, attendance tracking, mobile apps for parents, and billing.21 Crucially, it handles "make-up classes" (odrabianie zajęć)—a specific pain point in the Polish market where parents expect value retention for missed classes due to illness.  
* **Pricing Model:** It employs a tiered model. There is a **Free Plan** for limited features, and **Premium Plans** scale with student count (e.g., \~125 PLN/month for 50 students) and SMS limits (100-1500 SMS).22  
* **Strategic Moat:** The "ActiveNow" brand is visible on the client-facing booking pages of schools like ChlorekSwim.16 This creates a network effect where parents become accustomed to the interface.

### **4.2 SportsManago: The Vertical Specialist**

SportsManago focuses intensely on team sports (football, judo, karate), distinguishing itself with features irrelevant to language schools but vital for clubs.

* **Feature Set:** It includes modules for **medical examinations** (badań lekarskich), licensing, and team management.24 In Poland, competitive youth sports require strict adherence to medical clearance, making this feature a critical differentiator.  
* **Pricing:** Aggressive entry pricing (\~39 EUR/month or 150 PLN netto), often bundling "Unlimited SMS".25 This targets the communication-heavy nature of sports coaching (game changes, weather cancellations).  
* **Market Presence:** Used by KS Olimp (Karate) in Łódź 26, proving its traction in the martial arts vertical.

### **4.3 LangLion: The Language Hegemon**

LangLion dominates the language school vertical.

* **Feature Set:** It goes beyond administration into *pedagogy*, offering e-gradebooks, e-learning modules, and library management.27  
* **Pricing:** A per-student model (approx. 1.50 \- 1.70 PLN per student/month) with a minimum monthly fee (\~115 PLN).28 This aligns costs with the school's revenue, making it attractive for growing schools.  
* **Lock-in:** Because it holds academic records and grades, displacing LangLion is incredibly difficult compared to replacing a simple booking tool.

### **4.4 Kidos.pl: The Marketplace Challenger**

Kidos represents a different strategic approach: the aggregator/marketplace model.

* **Business Model:** Instead of selling software to schools, Kidos aggregates listings to help parents find classes.30 It offers a partner program where schools can list empty spots to optimize revenue.32  
* **Limitations:** While strong in Warsaw and Lublin, its presence in Łódź appears weaker than the SaaS incumbents. Marketplaces suffer from the "chicken-and-egg" problem: without all the best schools (who often don't need marketing help because they are full), parents don't use the app. Without parents, schools don't list.

### **4.5 LiveKid: The Pivot Success Story**

LiveKid is the most important case study for any new entrant.

* **Origin:** Started as a communication app.  
* **Pivot:** Evolved into a comprehensive "Operating System" for preschools and nurseries, handling payments, catering orders, and facility management.4  
* **Scale:** Revenue estimated at 4.2M PLN (2024) with significant VC backing (\>5M USD).5  
* **Lesson:** LiveKid succeeded by moving *upstream* from simple communication to **financial operations** (payments/catering) in a high-frequency vertical (daily attendance). This contrasts with the weekly frequency of after-school activities.

## **5\. Problem Acuteness and Jobs to Be Done (JTBD)**

Understanding the "Tarpit" requires analyzing the gap between the user's problem and their willingness to pay for a solution.

### **5.1 The Administrative "Hair on Fire"**

For providers in Łódź, the administrative burden is real and acute.

* **Coordination Failure:** The "Product One-Pager" accurately identifies "coordination failure" as the core problem.35 Solo instructors spend disproportionate time forming groups, matching skill levels, and managing cancellations.  
* **The "Make-up" Loop:** In the Polish market, if a child misses a swim lesson, the parent expects a make-up slot. Managing this manually via SMS is chaotic. ActiveNow’s automated suggestion engine for make-up dates 21 directly addresses this, saving hours of admin time per week.

### **5.2 The Financial Pain Point: Debt Collection**

The most visceral pain point is **payment collection**.

* **Current State:** Many providers rely on manual bank transfers. They must check their bank account, match names (often the parent's name differs from the child's), and manually send SMS reminders to late payers.  
* **Regulatory Pressure:** Snippets regarding "regulatory changes" and consumer protection laws 36 suggest that schools are under pressure to have compliant contracts and transparent refund policies.  
* **JTBD:** The provider wants to "get paid on time without having awkward conversations with parents." This is a high-value problem. ActiveNow and SportsManago address this with "automatic reminders" 21, but few offer true *automated direct debit* or *guaranteed payments*, leaving a gap for fintech innovation.

### **5.3 The Consumer Friction**

Parents face a discovery and booking struggle.

* **Fragmentation:** Information is scattered across Facebook groups, school websites, and physical flyers.  
* **Booking Anxiety:** Parents want certainty. They want to know "is there a spot for my 7-year-old on Tuesdays at 17:00?" without calling. The rise of aggregators like Kidos.pl and Zarezerwuj.pl 32 attempts to solve this, but the inventory is rarely real-time unless the provider uses a connected SaaS.

## **6\. The "Tarpit" Assessment: Structural Risks**

Is the Łódź after-school market a tarpit? The evidence suggests **YES**, for a generic solution, but **NO** for a specialized one.

### **6.1 Why It Is a Tarpit (The Risks)**

1. **Fragmentation of Requirements:** A generic "booking tool" fails because vertical needs are too distinct. A language school (grades, textbooks) cannot use the same system as a judo club (medical exams, weight classes). Building a "one-size-fits-all" platform leads to feature bloat and a product that pleases no one.38  
2. **Low Willingness to Pay (WTP):** The "Minnows" (small providers) are extremely price-sensitive. With free tools like Google Calendar and WhatsApp available, the perceived value of a 100 PLN/month subscription is low unless it directly drives *revenue* (new students) rather than just saving time.  
3. **High Cost of Sales:** Selling to non-technical SMB owners requires high-touch onboarding. If a sales rep has to spend 5 hours closing a deal worth 1500 PLN/year, the unit economics are broken.39  
4. **The "Excel" Competitor:** For a small provider with 30 students, Excel or a physical notebook is "good enough." Displacing "good enough" is the classic definition of a SaaS tarpit.

### **6.2 Why It Might Be a Blue Ocean (The Opportunities)**

1. **Professionalization:** The market is professionalizing. Parents spending 2400 PLN/year expect a professional interface, mobile payments, and digital progress tracking.2 Providers who look "amateur" are losing business to those using ActiveNow or LangLion.  
2. **Fintech Wedge:** The pain of debt collection is universal. A platform that acts as the **Merchant of Record (MoR)**—collecting payments from parents via BLIK/Card and settling with the provider—solves the biggest pain point and justifies a higher "take rate" (e.g., 1-2% of GMV) compared to a flat SaaS fee.

## **7\. Strategic Pivot Analysis: From "Booking Tool" to "Vertical Fintech"**

Given the dominance of incumbents in the pure "management" space, a new entrant must pivot. The "LiveKid" model offers the blueprint.

### **7.1 The Pivot: Financial Operating System**

Instead of competing on "scheduling features" (where ActiveNow wins), a new platform should compete on **financial flow**.

* **Mechanism:** Offer the software for free (or very low cost) but mandate the use of the platform's payment processing. Monetize via a transaction fee on the tuition collected.  
* **Value Prop:** "We guarantee your cash flow. We chase the parents. We offer parents 'Buy Now, Pay Later' for semester fees."  
* **Feasibility in Łódź:** With semester swimming courses costing \~600 PLN 17, a BNPL option for parents (paying in 3 installments) while paying the school upfront would be a massive differentiator that ActiveNow currently does not center its value prop around.

### **7.2 The Pivot: The "Walled Garden" Network**

Leverage the physical infrastructure of Łódź.

* **Strategy:** Partner with the venues, not just the schools.  
* **Execution:** Approach "Aquapark Fala" or "Wodny Raj".16 Offer them a system to manage *their* lane rentals to the swim schools. In exchange, mandate that any school renting lanes must use your booking platform. This forces adoption top-down, bypassing the expensive sales cycle of pitching individual schools.

## **8\. Financial Modeling: Unit Economics in Łódź**

To validate the viability, we must construct a theoretical P\&L for a typical "Dolphin" client in Łódź.

**Scenario: Mid-Sized Swim School in Łódź**

* **Students:** 150 active students.  
* **Tuition:** 200 PLN / month.  
* **Monthly Revenue:** 30,000 PLN.  
* **Annual Revenue:** \~300,000 PLN (accounting for summer dip).

**Option A: SaaS Subscription (ActiveNow Model)**

* **Cost:** \~300 PLN/month (Premium tier).  
* **Cost to Client:** \~3,600 PLN/year (1.2% of revenue).  
* **Platform Revenue:** Low upside, capped by pricing tiers.

**Option B: Fintech / Transaction Model (Pivot Model)**

* **Cost:** 0 PLN subscription.  
* **Fee:** 1.5% transaction fee on payments.  
* **Cost to Client:** 450 PLN/month.  
* **Platform Revenue:** 5,400 PLN/year.  
* **Client Benefit:** "Free software" \+ automated debt collection. The 1.5% is often passed to the parent or absorbed as "cost of doing business" (cheaper than chasing bad debt).

The Transaction Model yields **50% higher revenue per client** and lowers the barrier to entry, avoiding the "subscription fatigue" tarpit.

## **9\. Conclusion and Recommendations**

The market for managing paid after-school activities in Poland is a **high-potential but structurally difficult** sector. It is not a green pasture waiting for a basic digital solution; it is a mature battlefield with entrenched vertical specialists.

**Verdict:** Entering the market as a generic "booking system" is a **TARPIT**. You will face high churn from small providers and insurmountable feature gaps against incumbents like ActiveNow (Swim) and LangLion (Languages).

**Viable Pivot:** The path to success lies in **specialization and financial integration**.

1. **Verticalize:** Do not build for "everyone." Build specifically for **one** underserved vertical in Łódź first (e.g., Dance/Arts, which is less digitized than Swim/Lang).  
2. **Monetize Money, Not Time:** Adopting a Fintech-enabled business model (transaction fees \+ financial services) aligns the platform with the provider's revenue growth and circumvents the reluctance to pay monthly subscriptions.  
3. **Hyper-Local Go-to-Market:** Use Łódź as a beachhead by partnering with facility owners (pools, gyms, cultural centers) to distribute the software top-down, rather than selling door-to-door to individual instructors.

By shifting focus from "organizing classes" to "optimizing revenue," a new platform can escape the SMB tarpit and build a scalable venture in the growing Polish education economy.

### **10.2 The "LiveKid" Phenomenon: A Roadmap?**

LiveKid's trajectory 4 is critical. They didn't just build a "better calendar." They solved the **communication trust** loop between parents and kindergartens (photos, meals, naps) and then layered **payments** on top.

* *Application to After-School:* The equivalent "trust loop" in after-school activities is **progress tracking**. Parents paying 2000 PLN/year for judo want to know: "Is my child actually getting better?" A platform that focuses on **skill progression tracking** (gamification, digital badges, video feedback) could replicate LiveKid's engagement model in the PASA sector.

### **10.3 The "Kidos.pl" Partner Model**

Research into Kidos 30 reveals they operate a "partner program" where schools list *empty* spots. This is a yield management play.

* *Weakness:* It assumes schools *have* empty spots they want to discount. High-demand schools in Łódź (like British Centre) are often oversubscribed.  
* *Opportunity:* A SaaS platform could integrate with Kidos (API) to automatically push unsold inventory to the marketplace, acting as the "channel manager" rather than just the booking engine.

### **10.4 Regulatory and Administrative Constraints**

The Polish legal environment adds friction that software must solve.

* **RODO (GDPR):** Handling data of minors requires strict compliance. ActiveNow emphasizes this.42 A new platform must be "RODO-secure" out of the box.  
* **Insurance (NNW):** Clubs often need to manage insurance for participants. SportsManago likely integrates this data.25  
* **Invoicing:** Integration with the Polish "KSeF" (National e-Invoicing System) will soon be mandatory. This is a massive "tarpit" feature—boring, hard to build, but legally required. Incumbents have a head start here.

### **10.5 Final Recommendation on the "Łódź Strategy"**

If proceeding with a launch in Łódź:

1. **Ignore the Language Schools:** They are locked in by LangLion.  
2. **Target the "Semi-Professional" Arts/Dance Sector:** This vertical in Łódź shows the lowest tech adoption (using generic forms or paper). They have high visual needs (Instagram integration) and "drop-in" complexities that ActiveNow serves less well than Sports/Swim.  
3. **Price for Cash Flow:** Launch with a "Pay-as-you-grow" transaction model. Do not charge a setup fee. Win the trust of the "Minnows" and grow with them as they become "Dolphins."

The data is clear: The demand is there, but the supply side is a minefield of specialized needs. Success requires deep vertical focus, not broad horizontal ambition.

### **Kluczowe Założenia i Korekty dla Dzielnic**

Poniżej wyjaśniam logikę przyjętych korekt dla poszczególnych obszarów, abyś mógł lepiej zrozumieć dynamikę miasta:

#### **1\. Bałuty (Mnożnik x1.45 względem Polesia)**

To demograficzny hegemon. Bałuty są niemal o 50% ludniejsze od Polesia.

* **Specyfika:** Dzielnica posiada unikalną enklawę – **Julianów**. Jest to jedna z najbogatszych części Łodzi (wille). Tamtejszy klient podbija średni przychód na dziecko (ARPU), wybierając drogie zajęcia (tenis, jazda konna w Łagiewnikach, prywatne szkoły muzyczne). To rekompensuje niższą siłę nabywczą "wielkiej płyty" na Teofilowie czy Wielkopolsce.

#### **2\. Widzew (Mnożnik x1.10 względem Polesia mimo tej samej populacji)**

Mimo że Widzew ma oficjalnie tyle samo mieszkańców co Polesie, jego rynek edukacyjny może być **nieznacznie większy**.

#### **3\. Śródmieście (Mnożnik x0.40 – Specjalny Przypadek)**

Matematyczne wyliczenie z populacji dałoby ok. 20 mln PLN, ale realny popyt generowany przez *mieszkańców* jest niższy.

* **Problem:** Śródmieście się wyludnia, dominują single, studenci i seniorzy.  
* **Paradoks "Importera":** Śródmieście ma wysokie przychody *placówek* (np. British Centre, szkoły muzyczne przy ul. Sosnowej), ale pieniądze te przywożą rodzice z Widzewa, Polesia i Bałut. Jako rynek *zamieszkania*, Śródmieście jest najsłabsze.

#### **4\. Górna (Mnożnik x1.15)**

Dzielnica bardzo zbliżona strukturą do Polesia, ale z mniejszą liczbą prestiżowych "kotwic" (jak Aquapark Fala czy Złotno).

* **Struktura:** Ruda Pabianicka (bogatsza, willowa) równoważy biedniejsze rejony Chojen. Popyt jest stabilny, ale oferta jest często tańsza, bardziej lokalna i mniej "prestiżowa" niż na Polesiu, co może nieznacznie obniżać średnie wydatki na dziecko.
